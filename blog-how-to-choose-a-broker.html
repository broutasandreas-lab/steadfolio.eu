How to Choose a Brokerage Account: What Actually Matters

6 min read - Educational content, not financial advice

Before you can buy your first investment, you need a brokerage account - the platform that actually executes your trades. With so many options advertising themselves as "the best," here's what's actually worth comparing.

1. Regulation and investor protection

Check that the broker is regulated by a recognized financial authority in the country or region where it operates, and understand what investor protection scheme applies to your holdings if the broker itself ever ran into trouble. This is worth confirming before anything else on this list.

2. Fees - and where they hide

"Commission-free" doesn't always mean genuinely free. Look at the full picture:

  • Trading commissions (even if advertised as zero, confirm it)
  • Currency conversion fees, especially if investing in assets priced in a different currency
  • Account maintenance or inactivity fees
  • Withdrawal fees
Worth remembering: As discussed in payment for order flow (see our article on how markets actually work), some "free" brokers generate revenue in less visible ways. Free isn't necessarily bad - just worth understanding.

3. What you can actually invest in

Not every broker offers access to every market, fund, or fractional shares. If you already know you want to invest in specific ETFs or specific stock exchanges, confirm the broker actually supports them before signing up.

4. Usability

A platform that feels confusing or overwhelming can quietly discourage you from actually following through on your investing plan. This is a real, practical factor - not just a "nice to have."

5. Where your money and assets are actually held

Understand whether your investments are held in your own name (or a clearly segregated account) versus commingled in ways that are harder to trace. This connects back to the regulation and protection question above.

What matters less than people assume

Flashy features, social trading feeds, or gamified interfaces don't make a broker fundamentally better for long-term investing - and in some cases can encourage more frequent trading than is actually useful for a long-term strategy.

The takeaway

There's rarely a single "best" broker for everyone - the right choice depends on what you're trying to invest in, where you live, and what fee structure fits your situation. Steadfolio doesn't recommend a specific broker; the goal here is to help you know what questions to ask before choosing one yourself.
